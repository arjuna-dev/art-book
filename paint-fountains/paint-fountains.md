# Gemini Mixture 1

1. The Pigment (For Opacity)
   What to Buy: Titanium Dioxide (TiO₂) Powder

Why: This is the most common, non-toxic, and brilliantly opaque white pigment on Earth. It's used in everything from food coloring (to make things opaque) to house paint and sunscreen. It is a very fine, inert powder that will not dissolve or react. It will just "float" in your water, making it look like milk.

Where to find it: You can buy it online from art pigment suppliers, soap-making suppliers, or cosmetic-making suppliers.

2. The Dye (For Color)
   What to Buy: Standard Liquid Food Coloring (e.g., McCormick, AmeriColor)

Why: You still need a water-soluble dye for the color. This dye will dissolve in the water and color it, while the pigment just floats in it.

# chatGPT Build 1

## Water / “acrylic” phase

- **Base:** distilled water + **acrylic gloss medium**
- **Body:** a pinch of **xanthan gum** 0.1–0.3 percent by weight to dial in creamy viscosity
- **Pigment:** **water-dispersible pigment dispersion** or acrylic ink. Pick high-opacity whites or colors for that paint feel.
- Optional: a drop of preservative to prevent funk.

## Oil / “oil paint” phase

- **Base:** **polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) silicone oil** 100–350 cSt
- **Body:** **fumed silica (Aerosil/Cab-O-Sil)** 2–5 percent to make it buttery like artist oil
- **Pigment:** **oil-dispersible pigment paste** or pre-dispersed cosmetic pigments. Avoid solventy artist tubes; many carry mineral spirits that attack plastics and create stable emulsions.
- Tip: use oleophilic-treated TiO₂ or iron oxides for opacity.

Why this works: silicone oil is non-flammable, pump-friendly, and **immiscible** with your acrylic medium. Pigments stay in their own phase because you’re using **phase-appropriate dispersions**.

# Will they separate after “mixing” in the show zone?

Yes — as long as you **don’t add emulsifiers**. Turbulence may form temporary droplets, but in the settling tank the oil rises and the water sinks, then you skim/underflow them back to their loops. If you want a brief “milky” moment that still breaks:

- Add **a trace of low-HLB surfactant to the _oil_ only** (e.g., ~0.02 percent Span-type). Test first — too much creates a stubborn emulsion.
- Or skip surfactants and use **coalescer media** (oleophilic polypropylene pads or stainless knit mesh) in the separator to accelerate droplet merging without chemistry.

# Small prototype you can build this week

**Parts**

- Two **peristaltic pumps** with adjustable flow
- Clear **acrylic or borosilicate column** or a thin weir for sheet flow
- **Y-shaped co-flow nozzle** or adjacent lips for side-by-side laminar sheets
- **Separator basin** with two lanes: an upper **skimmer weir** for oil, a lower **underflow** for water
- **Coalescer pad** in the basin’s middle wall
- Tubing: silicone or PTFE
- Inline **200–400 μm screens** to catch pigment clumps
- LED backlight

**Steps**

1. **Mix phases** separately. Aim for **similar viscosities** so they glide together. Adjust xanthan or fumed silica until both pour like heavy cream to soft yogurt.
2. **Density tune:** if the oil rises too quickly, use **higher-viscosity** silicone or add a little **glycerin** to the water phase to close the density gap and slow separation to a pleasing pace.
3. **Co-flow the streams** into the display zone. Keep Reynolds numbers low for **laminar ribbons**; increase flow for dramatic tendrils.
4. **Collect in the basin.** The oil layer skims over a weir to its return pump. The water exits below a baffle to its pump. Coalescer media between zones speeds clarity.
5. **Cycle and tweak.** If you see persistent haze, reduce turbulence, raise temperature slightly, or remove any surfactant traces.
